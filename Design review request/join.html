Featured sub-study · Weaning diary

Keep a short food diary through the weaning months

Babies move from milk feeding to family meals between roughly 6 and 12 months — and the choices families make in that window shape gut development, food preferences, and even allergy risk for years to come. For this sub-study we're asking parents to keep a light-touch food and texture diary across a 4-week stretch of that transition.

"Most days take 2–3 minutes in the app. Tick what was offered, what was eaten, and how it went. We do the rest of the work." C-GULL nutrition team

What this could help us answer

Three open questions in early-life nutrition, and the research your diary builds on.

Gut microbiome

How does early food variety shape a baby's gut bacteria?

Recent work suggests that greater plant variety in complementary feeding speeds up gut-microbiome maturation, and that the response to specific solids depends on whether a baby was breastfed or formula-fed. The patterns are clear in small studies — your real-world food logs help us test them in a Liverpool cohort big enough to matter.

See: Dietary plant diversity predicts early life microbiome maturation, medRxiv (2025); Gut microbiota responses to complementary food sources differ by milk feeding type, Clinical Nutrition (2026).

Allergy prevention

When and how should families introduce common allergens?

The landmark LEAP trial showed that early introduction of peanut sharply reduces later peanut allergy across high- and low-risk infants. We still know little about when families actually introduce eggs, peanuts, sesame and dairy in everyday life, or what shapes those decisions — your diary helps connect the science to real practice.

See: Logan et al., Early introduction of peanut reduces peanut allergy across risk groups, Allergy (2023); Fisher et al., The challenges of preventing food allergy: Lessons learned from LEAP and EAT, Ann Allergy Asthma Immunol (2018).

Eating behaviour

Does baby-led or spoon-fed weaning affect later eating?

The BLISS randomised trial found a modified baby-led approach can support self-regulated eating without compromising iron intake — but its longer-term effects on food preferences and weight are still open. Diaries that capture how foods are offered, not just what, give us the data to follow those trajectories.

See: BLISS study protocol, BMC Pediatrics (2015); Effect of a Baby-Led Approach on Infant Growth and Overweight, JAMA Pediatrics (2017); Impact on iron intake and status, BMJ Open (2018).

How sub-studies work

Where you'll see them
Each open sub-study appears in this app under "Choose what to join", with a plain-English summary up front.
How long they take
From a single 10-minute questionnaire to diaries lasting a few weeks. Always stated before you commit.
Saying yes or no
You can opt in, decline, or step away mid-study at any point. It never affects the main C-GULL study.
Ethics & oversight
Every sub-study has its own ethics approval, listed on the invitation. The C-GULL team holds the data; partners only see de-identified results.
What you get back
A short plain-English summary once the study reports — usually within a year of it closing.
